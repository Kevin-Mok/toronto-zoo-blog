# White Lion Follow-Up Checklist

Use this on the next Toronto Zoo white lion talk so the blog can move from transcript-led reporting to stronger evidence-backed habitat and welfare coverage.

## Evidence Capture

- Take one full-width habitat photo from every public viewing angle.
- Take one medium shot that shows the main resting area, barriers, and visitor sightline together.
- Take close photos of substrate transitions: dirt, mulch, grass, rock, concrete, heated rock, den threshold, and any worn pacing path.
- Photograph all visible enrichment objects, browse, scent points, feeders, scratch or rub surfaces, toys, hanging items, and hidden-food devices.
- Photograph indoor access points, doors, caves, shaded zones, privacy screens, and sheltered retreat areas.
- Photograph posted signs with lion names, ages, talk times, conservation language, or recent updates.
- If lions are visible, capture body posture, gait, rest position, social spacing, and how much of the enclosure they actually use.
- If lions are not visible, photograph the empty habitat anyway so space, structure, and visitor interpretation can still be documented.

## Habitat Sufficiency Checks

- Estimate how much usable ground area is visible versus decorative or inaccessible space.
- Note whether the enclosure offers multiple activity zones rather than one main open pad.
- Check whether substrate looks varied enough for different lying, walking, and scenting behavior.
- Check whether there are enough elevation changes, visual breaks, and retreat options for older animals.
- Note whether enrichment looks fresh, rotated, or static.
- Watch for repeated route use that may suggest a habitual pacing line.
- Compare where lions choose to spend time versus where the habitat seems designed to draw them.

## Welfare and Behavior Checks

- Record which lion is visible: Lemon or Makali.
- Note whether the lion is resting, scanning, walking, rubbing, grooming, vocalizing, or interacting socially.
- Record whether both lions are together, separated by choice, or not simultaneously visible.
- Note any repeated back-and-forth path, fixated waiting at doors, or mesh-focused behavior.
- Note any obvious comfort signals: relaxed sprawl, side-sleeping, grooming, affiliative contact, or calm exploration.
- Note any possible concern signals without diagnosing cause: repetitive turning, prolonged door-watching, excessive waiting at one point, limping, favoring a limb, or persistent eye squinting.

## Questions To Confirm On Site

- If the lions are not out, is that usually weather, routine, or their own choice?
- Which parts of the habitat do they use most on a cold day?
- What enrichment did they get today or this week?
- About how warm is the house kept in winter (23?)?
- Is there a simple status update on Lemon/Makali today?
- Can you help me tell Lemon and Makali apart?
- What ground surfaces do they have access to outside and inside?
- Is there anything visitors often misread as stress that is actually routine for these lions?
- If I notice repeated squinting or another recurring trait, what is the safest way to describe that publicly?

## After You Collect More Evidence

- Review your photos first, then ask follow-up questions that point to something specific you saw.
- Bring one or two habitat photos that show usable space, substrate variety, and enrichment placement.
- Bring one note on route use, door-focused behavior, or repeated waiting if you observed it.
- Bring one note on which lion used which area of the enclosure.
- Use those specifics to ask Grant or Molly more detailed questions instead of asking broad hypotheticals.

## Follow-Up Targets By Person

- Floor keeper talk: start with the short questions above and keep the tone practical, brief, and easy to answer in public.
- Grant Furniss, Director of Wildlife Care:
  - After looking at your habitat photos, how does the zoo assess whether the visible public habitat is sufficient for two older adult females?
  - Have there been any age-related changes to care, access, or enclosure use that can be described on the record?
  - How should the blog accurately describe the lions' current institutional program status?
  - If visitors see limited enrichment from the public side, what rotating or behind-the-scenes context is important not to miss?
- Molly McGuire, Manager of Welfare Science:
  - Based on the behavior or route use I observed, what would count as routine movement versus a stereotypy concern?
  - What positive welfare indicators would you want a visitor or reporter to notice in this habitat?
  - How do you evaluate whether substrate, enrichment, and climate support are working well for these specific lions?
  - If a visitor notices recurring squinting or another physical trait, what is the safest evidence-based way to report it without implying an unconfirmed health problem?
