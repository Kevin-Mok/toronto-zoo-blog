# Goat Question Script

This is a reusable Toronto Zoo Report script built from the About page lens and tuned for a public goat talk or contact-yard setting. Use it to ask about welfare, habitat use, enrichment, visitor contact, and basic daily status without over-claiming or turning a public Q-and-A into a confrontation.

## Opening Script

Use a short setup that signals you want public, keeper-safe context.

`I'm documenting the goat area for Toronto Zoo Report. I'm mostly looking for public, keeper-talk context about welfare, habitat use, enrichment, and how visitor contact is managed fairly for the goats.`

## Direct Easy Questions For The Keeper

These are the fastest, most natural follow-up questions to ask on the floor.

- Can you help me tell them apart?
- If visitors can pet them, how do you tell when a goat wants contact versus wants space?
- If a goat steps away from visitors, how is that choice protected?
- What enrichment did they get today or this week?
- What enrichment do they respond to most?
- Is there a simple public-safe status update on them today?
- Which parts of the habitat do they use most on a cold day?
- What ground surfaces do they have access to here?
- Is there anything visitors often misread as stress, rudeness, or stubbornness that is actually normal goat behavior?

## If The Keeper Has Time, Go A Bit Deeper

These are still keeper-safe, but they take longer and work better after the quick questions.

### Welfare Signals

- What does a normal relaxed day look like for these goats?
- What behavior tells you they are comfortable with people?
- What behavior tells you they want less interaction or more distance?
- Are there behaviors visitors mistake for fear or irritation when they are actually routine?

### Touch and Visitor Management

- How do you decide which goats are in the contact area on a given day?
- What choices do the goats have if they do not want to be touched?
- Do they have retreat space or off-view space when they are done with people?
- How do staff intervene if visitors keep trying to pet a goat that is turning away?

### Substrates and Setup

- What ground surfaces do they have access to outside and inside?
- Does this habitat give them what they need for climbing, resting, hoof wear, and dry footing?
- Which parts of the habitat most resemble the kind of terrain goats need or prefer?
- On a cold day, which zones do they choose most often and why?

### Enrichment

- What enrichment did they get today or this week?
- Which enrichment gets the strongest response from this group?
- How often is enrichment rotated so it stays interesting?
- Are there browse, feeding, scent, rubbing, or climbing enrichments visitors usually miss?

### Social Management and Individual ID

- Can you help me tell the goats apart quickly?
- Who is most social with people, and who usually wants more space?
- Do certain goats usually stay together or claim favorite spots?
- Are there age, horn, coat, or personality differences visitors can safely use to identify them?

### Education and Framing

- What is the main educational purpose of having goats in this area?
- What should visitors learn from this habitat besides that the goats are friendly?
- Are there husbandry or welfare lessons here that also apply to other hoofstock species?

## Habitat Evidence Prompts For Yourself

- Ask the short keeper questions first, then get photos that support or challenge the answers.
- Get one frame showing the full public contact layout, one showing substrate, and one showing retreat or no-contact space.
- If petting is allowed, document how the space lets goats approach or leave on their own.
- Photograph browse, feeders, rubbing points, climbing structures, shelters, and any elevated resting spots.
- Note which goats approach people, which avoid people, and whether staff reinforce boundaries when a goat turns away.

## After You Collect More Info, Escalate To Management Or Welfare Staff

Use these after you have photos, notes, and a few concrete observations. These work better when you can point to something you saw instead of asking in the abstract.

### Grant Furniss, Director of Wildlife Care

- How does Toronto Zoo assess whether a contact-yard setup gives goats enough choice and control over visitor interaction?
- What counts as enough usable space, substrate variety, shelter, and climbing opportunity for this group?
- How are cold-weather use patterns factored into habitat design and daily management?
- If visitors mainly notice petting, what behind-the-scenes care or enrichment context is important so reporting stays fair?

### Molly McGuire, Manager of Welfare Science

- What welfare indicators matter most in a habitat where visitor contact is part of the experience?
- How do you distinguish normal approach-and-avoid behavior from overstimulation or interaction fatigue?
- What observations would show that the goats have meaningful control over whether they engage with people?
- How do you assess whether substrate, space, and enrichment are working well for these individuals?

## Status And Health Questions That May Be Deflected

Ask these only when you have the right contact and on-record permission.

- Is there any publicly shareable medical or husbandry update on specific goats today?
- Are any goats currently being given less visitor contact for welfare, age, or health reasons?
- Have there been any recent changes in hoof care, enrichment, social grouping, or habitat access that visitors should understand?

## Close

`Thanks. I'm trying to keep the write-up grounded in public observations and keeper-safe context, so I'll route any more sensitive health or welfare-review questions to management or welfare staff.`
