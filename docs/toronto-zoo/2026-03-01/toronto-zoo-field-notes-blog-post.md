## Western Lowland Gorilla Talk
The species transcripts describe active social management rather than one static troop view. Keepers distinguish between a bachelor grouping and a family grouping, and they avoid putting males together directly because that can escalate into fights.

Introductions are staged in steps: first with two barriers, then with one wire barrier, so gorillas can see and acclimate before closer integration. The transcript repeatedly frames this as a gradual process based on behavior signals rather than a one-time release.

Visitors may see different groups depending on the time of day and season. The transcript notes half-day rotation between boys and girls and seasonal outside/inside rotation, which explains why viewing can change between passes.

## White Lion Talk
Lion transcripts note that the white lions were in-house during part of this window, so visitors could not always see them at the glass. Keepers described indoor access around 23C and emphasized gradual temperature transitions when animals move between indoor and outdoor spaces.

Family context in the transcripts identifies two females, Lemon and Makali (called Mac), discussed as long-term companions around 15 years old. The same conversation references the male Fintan and gives lifespan framing: roughly 10-14 years in the wild and up to about 20 years in human care.

Conservation context in the keeper discussion states these are white lions, not albino lions, and explains the white coat as a genetic trait requiring both parents to carry the relevant gene. The transcript also links this rarity to poaching and trophy-hunting pressure in their regional range.

## Preservation Lens
